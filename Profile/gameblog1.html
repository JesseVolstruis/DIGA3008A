An analysis on Celeste's level design

By Jesse Schroder

30 April 2025

Background:

Celeste is an indie 2D precision platformer that originally started as a game jam project. It was later made into a full title. The game has you climb Celeste Mountain in the form of 7 chapters. (9 if you include bonus content) Each chapter has the player progress through various platforming challenges as you climb the mountain. Your main movement mechanics are movement, jumping, the ability to cling to and climb walls and most importantly, a dash that functions in the 8 cardinal directions.

Level Structure:

Each chapter is distinct whether it be in visuals, narrative or chapter specific level elements, each follow a broad structure. (a blueprint of sorts) In Celeste, each chapter is comprised of individual screens that the player must solve. (overcome that screen's challenges and make it to the end) A chapter's structure is like a story: Beginning: The player scopes out the level, familiarizes themselves with its elements and themes.

Tension: A problem is presented for the player to solve

Climax: A more difficult problem or an escalation of the previous one

Resolution: a reward, whether that be progression or a collectible

This could be as small as the structure for a single screen, with the introduction of a problem, the player solving it, and progressing as a reward. It could also apply to the chapter as a whole, with the introduction of a level element, increasingly difficult challenges involving it, and completing the chapter as a resolution. It can also be used multiple times, such as each third of a chapter having its own beginning, increase in tension and resolution. This structure is intertwined with narrative and gameplay. (especially regarding difficulty)

Difficulty:

Celeste strikes a perfect balance with its difficulty. Not too easy as to bore the player, but not too challenging to frustrate them. How this is achieved is by balancing the length of a segment of gameplay and how demanding that segment is. A challenge is less demanding if the player has less to keep track of or less actions to take to avoid being killed. An example would be flat stable ground being considerably less demanding than a piece of ground that disappears shortly after standing on it. It could also be the number of actions needed for the challenge, such as a jump requiring either one dash or multiple in succession.

The length of the screen plays a part as well. One way is stakes. The longer the screen, the more progress is lost when you fail. Another way is vision. In smaller screens, the player can see more of the problems they need to solve without moving.

Referring to the structure, a short screen with less demanding challenges is great for points of low tension. The jumps are easy and there aren't many of them. This is typically used for level introductions or screens where the player immediately applies what they've learned previously. Screens that are short but contain very demanding jumps are great for increasing difficulty without becoming frustrating, or are good for enforcing the mechanics that are being taught in that chapter, acting as quick spikes in tension. The challenge is high but barely any progress is lost on failure so the player can learn quicker. These are also used for optional content for players that want more difficult challenges while not being mandatory for less skilled players. Long screens that use less demanding jumps are great for testing what players have learned. A long series of simple jumps that feel very satisfying when done correctly. This could also be used to created more stressful situations as more progress is lost on death. These screens are usually the climax of a self-contained segment in the chapter. These are all used in combination, creating rising and falling tension and challenge which creates the flow states players can enter when playing the game. Achieving a balance in length and challenge is how Celeste achieves its satisfying level of difficulty. This is supported by the creator, saying that screens that are hard to understand are easy to execute and screens that are easy to understand are hard to execute.

Level elements:

What makes each chapter distinct (other than its narrative and visuals) are chapter specific level elements. These are directly related the player's movement. Some are based on movement, activating when the player does an action. Some directly alter how you move such as a spring propelling you upwards or moving obstacles you need to time your movement to. How these elements are used determines the problems the player needs to overcome.

How Celeste uses these elements effectively is by squeezing as much out of each when it comes to using them for problem solving. Looking at chapter 1's traffic blocks as an example, at first you only need to use their direct movement to solve the problem. Later on, you need to use them their movement for extra momentum for your jumps. The way the element works is not changed, only how you're using it.

This is how Celeste creates a cycle of learning. You're introduced to an element, you learn how it works and later you apply your understanding of these elements to solve multiple types of problems. What makes this satisfying is that players can initially struggle to learn a mechanic however, once they do, successfully applying your knowledge to solve problems makes players feel fulfilled and satisfied.

Narrative:

What makes Celeste's level design successful is how its linked to the narrative. The overarching narrative is that the player character (Madeline) is challenging herself to climb a mountain on a journey of self-discovery. The challenges in this journey are represented by the challenges in the levels. The act of overcoming the platforming challenges is linked to climbing the mountain. The challenging gameplay relates perseverance in the face of adversity to the various struggles with mental health as the game's underlying themes are about anxiety and depression. The game also uses a more direct way of linking these struggles to the gameplay in the form of Badeline: a reflection of the player character's struggles in physical form that the player must confront.
