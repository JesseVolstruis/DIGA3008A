Capitec's UI/UX

  • Capitec

By Jesse Schroder

21 Apr 2025

An analysis on the ethics of Capitec Bank's UI/UX practices:

Introduction:

UI and UX ethics play a key role in how users interact with websites. Due to the sensitivity of the services being provided, financial institutes such as Capitec Bank face certain ethical challenges with their web design. UI and UX can be used to manipulate users in a variety of ways, the website should not only be functional, but also responsible in how it uses UI and UX. This essay will analyse the UI/UX of Capitec Bank's website and the ethics in its design choices. By analysing the site's use of principles regarding usability, transparency, accessibility this essay will explore the user experience and underlying ethical choices in Capitec's website design. This analysis will showcase Capitec's strengths and weaknesses in creating a user-friendly website as well investigating how it delivers its digital service.

UI and UX analysis:

Usability:

Regarding the website's home page, the usability of Capitec's website appears functional at a glance and displays intuitive design in areas however, a user's experience with the site heavily depends on their pre-existing knowledge of online banking. The page has a strong visual hierarchy. Large text displayed prominently on the page draws the user's eye. This guides users to key buttons for downloading and switching to Capitec. The language used is simple and promotes the company: “Imagine banking that works for you…”. By making use of text and buttons that clearly stand out, Capitec effectively displays its core mission and its key navigation buttons. (such as downloading the app and switching banks) This is a positive use of Gestalt design regarding visual hierarchy and proximity [1]. The site uses the Von Restorff Effect by using visual differences among the buttons to make certain options more visible. The contrasted buttons are related to access [2]. However, the density of the content on the homepage may increase the cognitive load on users and lead to confusion, especially those not familiar with online banking. This relates to Hick's law about the number of choices affecting the decision-making time of users [3]. This shows that, while Capitec demonstrates ethical use of visual hierarchy on its homepage, the density of its content can hinder usability if a user is not already familiar with online banking.

Regarding the navigation, it is somewhat intuitive however, the quantity of navigation menus may be overwhelming. The main navigation bar has four distinct options: Transact, Save, Insure, and Credit. These are visible without having to scroll, as well as a secondary dropdown navigation menu under the “menu” tab. This reveals slightly more advanced options: Investor relations, Media centre and Help centre. Additionally, there is a search bar to aid with navigation. The presence of multiple navigation menus may lead to confusion and emphasize Hick's law regarding too many navigation options. However, using the more concealed navigation menu for more advanced options is an effective use of the Progressive Disclosure design principle where information is gradually revealed to reduce confusion. [4] By making use of these techniques, the site's navigation does not actively hinder usability and are in line with ethical UI and UX practices regarding clarity. However, due to the amount of navigation choices, users with less experience may suffer more cognitive load and be less efficient.

The login button is easy to locate at the site's top right corner. However, the button is understated compared to the marketing related navigation options which could conflict with user's needs. The login button's location is a common place for a login button on many sites and is predictable by users. The information presented to the user for logging in is concise and asks the user whether it is a business account or personal account before asking for account number and username. However, the login button does not stand out as much as the buttons promoting the app and the option to switch to Capitec. The marketing related buttons have a contrasting background colour to their neighbouring buttons and stand out as a result using the Von Restorff Effect [2]. The simple, linear login process promotes usability and is especially important with a service as essential as banking however, the prioritization of marketing may conflict with this usability and reflect negatively on the website's UI and UX practices.

The site's mobile layout as well as choice of font promote accessibility but shares the same problems regarding user experience. The mobile layout prioritizes the same information as desktop and is responsive with load times. The main navigation bar is fixed to the bottom of the screen and is easily locatable. The dropdown menu is kept at the top of the screen with its options intact. The login button is easily viewable at the top of the screen and the same contrasting buttons are used for navigation options. By following the same design techniques in both the mobile and desktop layout, there is no significant advantage in using one over the other. These options boost accessibility lining up with ethical UI and UX principles.

While the site has effective usability for experienced users, it may not be intuitive to those who are unfamiliar with online banking. Banking is an essential and sensitive service. Because of this the site's accessibility may suffer from design choices aimed at experienced users. This highlights how even well-designed websites can raise ethical concerns when balance is not considered between demographics.

Transparency:

Capitec's website structure is effective in presenting its core functions. More complex content, such as interest rates and eligibility requirements are not immediately viewable and clear which may hinder transparency. The core functions according to the navigation bar and home page are: Transact, Save, Insure, and Credit. These are detailed further on the page. Scrolling down gives a brief description of each of these functions. More complex options are contained in a dropdown menu and require more clicks to access. While terms such as “Insure” and “Save” are easy to understand, options such as “Social Impact” and “Media centre” have less immediate clarity and users may have a harder time using the services outside of the four main options. However, revealing the more complex information gradually rather than immediately is a responsible use of the Progressive Disclosure principle preventing users from becoming overwhelmed [4]. These demonstrate an effective use of design principles regarding the transparency of the site's navigation options. Providing clarity for its key services. Despite lacking immediate clarity, the site's more complex navigation options are concealed as to not overwhelm users when balancing transparency and content density.

While the website clearly presents key functions such as viewing fees, signing, and switching banks, the option to open an account is not as accessible which may lead to user distrust. The homepage clearly labels options essential in online banking. This can be seen in the option to view your fees and the option to switch banks being prioritized over neighbouring buttons by making use of contrasting backgrounds. However, the option to open an account can only be found near the bottom of most pages and is not as clearly presented. This raises an ethical concern of purposefully de-emphasizing this essential action. This is an example of obstruction, which involves deceptive patterns that delay or discourage user actions [5] This can negatively impact user trust and the site's clarity.

While the website's search bar can provide helpful results, the lack of suggested search terms and autocomplete can hinder users in effectively searching for information. The search bar is useful in aiding confused users by providing multiple results that contain your search query. However, there is a lack of varied suggested search options. Under “popular searches” are three options regarding the mobile app. There is a lack in clarification between these three options which may further confuse users. Additionally, there is no autocomplete or suggestion when entering search queries which is a highly recommended feature of search usability [6]. This details a lack of balance between clarity and marketing. With the marketing of the mobile app being prioritized over a more in-depth search function hindering transparency.

Regarding the ethics in a financial context, Banking websites are ethically obligated to ensure their interfaces are clear and promote trust [7]. Although the website has effective clarity with respect to its navigation options and key services, the de-emphasis of the account opening option and priority on marketing the mobile app may dissuade user trust and negatively impact the site's transparency.

Accessibility:

Capitec's website has strong accessibility focused design in areas such as screen reader support, responsive mobile layout, keyboard navigation, and readability. Capitec has stated their desire to improve accessibility regarding those with impaired visibility and has collaborated with a blind computer programmer [8]. However, there is no explicit statement on the compatibility with certain screen readers. The website supports keyboard navigation. Users can use tab to navigate between interactable elements and the arrow keys can be used for scrolling. The presence of keyboard navigation lines up with the global standard for digital accessibility [9]. The site uses a readable font with an above average font size. The colours present on the website create an effective contrast when used for its interfaces. (for example, its buttons) According to the WAVE web accessibility evaluation tool, no contrast errors are present [10]. These demonstrate ethical use of accessibility in the website's design.

Conclusion:

From the above we can see that Capitec's website shows a strong understanding of ethical UI and UX design. This can be seen in its visual hierarchy and responsive layout. The design supports usability, clarity and transparency with users who are already experienced with online banking. The site also promotes accessibility through its use of readable fonts, clear contrast, and option for keyboard navigation. However, the website's priority of marketing the mobile app over transparency in some areas such as account creation and search usability, as well as being potentially confusing and overwhelming for inexperienced bankers presents ethical concerns that may negatively impact user trust and site clarity which play essential roles in financial services. Overall while Capitec's site is largely responsible in its use of UI and UX, it lacks polish and refinement some of its ethical practices regarding transparency and usability.
