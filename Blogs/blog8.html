By Jesse Schroder

5 May 2025

Reading:

Digital inequalities in the age of artificial intelligence and big data by Christoph Lutz

Lutz introduces the concept of digital inequalities and that they're not just about access to technology but also refer to issues more deeply intertwined social inequalities like class, race, and gender. He notes that early digital inequality research focused on internet access and skills, revealing that disadvantaged groups remain disadvantaged online. This article refers to 3 levels: access to technology, digital skills and use, and outcomes and benefits from technology. Lutz points out that as new technologies like AI, big data, and algorithms emerge, digital inequality research have had less presence in the discussion. This introduction resonates with how quickly technological narratives can leave social voices underrepresented. Lutz's concern about digital inequalities research is important, as if we don't examine who benefits from or is harmed by AI and big data, we may continue reinforcing oppression. It's not just about joining the conversation but changing it, making sure digital equality is a central concern. His mentioning of needing an open approach also reflects a growing need in academics about not only questioning technology but examining why we use it and who it serves.

First level divide:

The first level divide refers to disparities in access to the internet originally focused on who could and couldn't connect to new technologies. Significant access gaps were revealed among different demographics. While divides in wealthier locations (like the U.S) have narrowed as availability grew, some other divides persist especially in rural areas. Overall, access is still unequal. While internet access is becoming more widespread, access no longer just refers to connection but about how people connect and what they can do online such as mobile users having smaller screens and slower speeds. I agree with access not being equal even with widespread internet. The comments about mobile access being second-class internet access is also emphasized in the reading. These technologies can also deepen the divide. An example would be a community pushed towards mobile internet access falling behind because of a potentially inferior way of accessing the internet. This could undermine the ideals of the internet as an open and participatory space.

Second level divide:

The second level divide shifts the focus away from simply accessing the internet, nut focuses on what people do with that access or their skills. Research examined the differences in digital skill between along various factors such as: age, gender, education level. Some studies found that young, educated men are among the more active internet users. Research also reveals divisions relating to participation such as creating content or engaging in discussion. Age seems to be a string factor in determining participation. Additionally certain platforms seem to be gendered reinforcing the social divide. I think that while access alone speaks about social inequality, it is further reinforced by examining what people can do with that access, what they can gain. I see the second divide as deeper kind of inequality, one that's built on preexisting power structures.

Third level divide:

The third level divide focuses on the outcomes people experience on the internet even with relatively equal access and usage skills. These can be economic outcomes, social, or health related. This divide is shaped by user's skills, attitude, and the types of online activities they do. Age also plays a factor but with less consistent effects. It is important to analyse both benefits and harms of internet use. The third divide includes not only those who benefit, but also who is more exposed to risk which can affect some online communities more greatly than others. I think that the third divide only reinforces the idea that the internet is not inherently empowering but rather, only amplifies certain existing social structures. The wealthy and well educated are more likely to reap the benefits of the internet where vulnerable groups remain as such.
